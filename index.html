What the paper examines

The article connects the Brexit debate with value-added trade accounting, focusing on how production linkages are organized across countries and industries.

Research question

Have non-EU countries become more involved in the UK’s value-added trade following the EU referendum?

Data and method

The study uses Asian Development Bank multiregional input-output tables for 2000–2023 to trace value-added flows.

Economic lens

Rather than relying only on gross trade, the paper examines direct and indirect value-added flows within global value chains.

Non-EU involvement increased, but EU centrality did not disappear.

Plain-language takeaway

Brexit raised an important question for UK trade policy: did the UK become less dependent on the EU and more connected with non-EU partners? This article approaches the question through value-added trade, which captures where economic value is created and how it moves through global production networks.

The findings suggest that several non-EU partners became more involved in the UK’s value-added trade after the referendum. However, this did not amount to a simple displacement of the EU. The EU remained central to the UK’s value-added trading activities, while indirect value-added trade through third countries gradually increased after formal EU withdrawal.
